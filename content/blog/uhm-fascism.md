## Intro

In his nominal work [The Ur Fascism](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/umberto-eco-ur-fascism) Umberto Eco lists fourteen different principles:

1. Cult of tradition
2. Traditionalism and rejection of modernism
3. Irrationalism and cult of action for action sake
4. Disagreement is Treason
5. Fear of difference
6. Appeal to a frustrated middle class
7. Obsession with a plot
8. Enemies need to be both too weak and too strong at the time
9. Life is a permanent warfare
10. Elitism and Contempt for the weak
11. Everybody is a Hero
12. Machismo
13. Selective populism aka The leader being the voice of the People
14. Newspeak

## TL;DR 

`Fascism is ultra-nationalist militaristic totalitarianism.` Anti-communism also applies, but modern versions don't have to compete with it.

## Why is Umberto Eco's Definition Wrong?

Ok, but why does the Umberto Eco's definition fail? 

Well for one, it's not a good enumerator of what is core of fascistic tenets, and second it makes a too wide of a net in it's definition.

And frankly when you get to it this is what author says himself:

> [!quote]
> Fascism was a fuzzy totalitarianism, a collage of different philosophical and political ideas, a beehive of contradictions.    
> \.\.\.  
> But in spite of this fuzziness, I think it is possible to outline a list of features that are typical of what I would like to call Ur-Fascism, or Eternal Fascism. These features cannot be organized into a system; many of them contradict each other, and are also typical of other kinds of despotism or fanaticism. But it is enough that one of them be present to allow fascism to coagulate around it. 

The fuzziness is the problem, many of the traits that correspond to fascism are present in many different systems, but they aren't fascist. Take for example Soviet Union, there you could find more instances of `Disagreement is Treason`, `Obsession with a plot`, `Appeal to a frustrated middle class`, `Irrationalism`, `Newspeak` and `Selective populism`, and so on, but was it any way fascistic? Not really.

## In Short

If you look deeply into these points you see a few common themes

- Totalitarianism: 4, 5, 6, 8, 13, 14
- Militarism: 3, 4, 7, 9, 10, 11, 12
- Ultra-nationalism: 1, 2, 3, 12

Which is what my succinit definition of it is. By removing excessive labels, you make the definiton more precise.

## View Within vs View Outside

I'm not the only or the first one to crticize Eco's definition. And though he might be a philosopher, many ancient philosophers also believed that there are only four basic elements, that there exists a universe of pure ideas, or that Sun rotates around Earth.

If Umberto Eco gave his definition of life, he'd probably claim that it is a fuzzy definition, and that in general life has claws, except it can have scales, wings and horns, but not always together and it's hard to tell whether it moves or is it still, and it changes over time, except when it doesn't. When in reality the succint definition of life is that it's carbon-based self replicating system of cells.[^1] 

Why did he made this definition? Perhaps for someone that lived during that time as a child, it's what fascism would have looked like. To paraphrase Lao Tzu *"Caught in fascism you see only manifestations."* It's a bit like watching a right-wing populist doing whatever he can to stay in power and assuming that all the contradictory things he says are what he thinks, when they are merely a survival mechanism.

Also from what I know, Umberto Eco didn't studied the thoughts of famous fascist/nazist as a historian would. Thus, he's also prone to his own biases.

## But Why Should One Care?

Why should one care about the definition of a fascist? We should be descriptivist[^2] not prescriptivist in our usage of language, I can hear you say. But by going by this road, we must then agree that in actual usage fascist means person I (strongly) disagree with. Much like democratic means good[^3].

And this is a notion that only benefits actual fascists.
In the same vein, Eco's fourteen criteria give everyone a plausible way to label anything as fascist. From Hitler, to Mao, to Bernie Sanders.
 
[^1]: For now at least.
[^2]: Prescriptivism in usage of a language, implies there is a correct and incorrect usage of language, while desrciptivism argues that language can only be described, not controlled.
[^3]: Ironically this was also pointed by Orwell, that in practice Democratic means good, and Fascist means bad, which is also a form of Newspeak.
